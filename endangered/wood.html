Wood Turtle

Glyptemys insculpta

Quick Facts

  • Conservation Status: Endangered (federally and provincially)
  • Size: 14-20 cm carapace length
  • Weight: 0.5-1 kg
  • Lifespan: Up to 40+ years in the wild
  • Distinctive Features: Sculpted shell, orange skin on neck/legs, intelligent behavior

Description

The Wood Turtle is a medium-sized freshwater turtle known for its highly sculpted, pyramid-like shell that resembles wood grain. The carapace is brown with yellow and black patterns, while the plastron (bottom shell) is yellow with dark blotches. Their skin is dark with distinctive bright orange coloration on the neck, legs, and tail.

Wood Turtles are considered one of the most intelligent turtle species, capable of solving simple problems and learning maze patterns. They exhibit a unique behavior called "worm stomping" where they rhythmically stamp their feet to lure earthworms to the surface.

Habitat and Range

Wood Turtles inhabit clean, slow-moving streams and rivers with sandy or gravelly bottoms in forested areas. They require both aquatic habitats for feeding and terrestrial habitats for nesting and summer foraging. In Ontario, they are found in scattered populations throughout the southern part of the province.

These turtles are semi-terrestrial, spending considerable time on land in forests, fields, and wetlands adjacent to their aquatic habitats. They hibernate in streams and rivers during winter, buried in mud or under banks.

Related habitats: Lake Ontario watershed, Algonquin Provincial Park region

Diet and Behavior

Wood Turtles are omnivorous, feeding on a varied diet including earthworms, insects, slugs, vegetation, berries, and fungi. Their unique "worm stomping" behavior demonstrates their problem-solving abilities as they create vibrations that mimic rainfall to lure earthworms to the surface.

They are active from April to October, with breeding occurring in spring and fall. Females nest in sandy or gravelly areas with good sun exposure, often traveling significant distances from water to find suitable nesting sites. Wood Turtles have small home ranges and show strong site fidelity.

Threats and Conservation

Wood Turtles face severe threats in Ontario:

  • Habitat Loss: Riverbank development, forest clearing, and wetland drainage
  • Road Mortality: High mortality when crossing roads between habitats
  • Nest Predation: High rates of nest destruction by predators
  • Illegal Collection: Targeted for the pet trade due to their intelligence and appearance
  • Agricultural Impacts: Habitat fragmentation and machinery-related deaths

Conservation efforts include habitat protection, road mitigation, public education, and nest protection programs. Several organizations are working to monitor and protect remaining populations.

Legal Protection

The Wood Turtle is listed as Endangered under both the federal Species at Risk Act (SARA) and Ontario's Endangered Species Act (ESA). This provides the highest level of protection, making it illegal to kill, harm, harass, capture, or possess this species.

Critical habitat protection is also mandated under SARA, requiring identification and protection of essential habitat features. The species is further protected under Ontario's Fish and Wildlife Conservation Act.
