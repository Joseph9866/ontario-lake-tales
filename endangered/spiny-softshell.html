Spiny Softshell Turtle

Apalone spinifera

Quick Facts

  • Conservation Status: Endangered (federally and provincially)
  • Size: 18-43 cm carapace length (females larger)
  • Weight: 1-11 kg
  • Lifespan: Up to 50+ years in the wild
  • Distinctive Features: Leathery shell, long snorkel-like nose, spiny projections on shell

Description

The Spiny Softshell Turtle is easily distinguished by its flat, leathery, pancake-like shell that lacks the hard scutes of other turtles. The carapace is olive to brown with dark blotches and small, spiny projections along the front edge. They have a long, tubular snout that functions like a snorkel, allowing them to breathe while mostly submerged.

Females are significantly larger than males, sometimes reaching over 40 cm in shell length. Their streamlined shape and soft shell make them excellent swimmers. The plastron is light-colored and flexible, providing little protection but allowing for greater mobility in water.

Habitat and Range

Spiny Softshell Turtles inhabit large rivers, lakes, and reservoirs with sandy or muddy bottoms, clean water, and abundant basking sites. They prefer areas with slow currents and are rarely found in small ponds or streams. In Ontario, they are restricted to a few locations in the southern part of the province.

These turtles spend most of their time in water, often burying themselves in soft substrates with only their head exposed. They require sandy beaches or banks for nesting. Spiny Softshell Turtles hibernate in mud at the bottom of water bodies during winter.

Related habitats: Lake Erie, Lake Ontario, Thames River system

Diet and Behavior

Spiny Softshell Turtles are primarily carnivorous, feeding on crayfish, insects, fish, amphibians, and other aquatic invertebrates. They are active hunters, using their excellent swimming abilities and speed to catch prey. Their long neck and snorkel-like nose allow them to ambush prey while remaining mostly hidden in substrate.

They are strong baskers and can often be seen sunning on logs, rocks, or banks. Breeding occurs in spring, and females nest in late spring to early summer on sandy beaches or banks. Females may travel significant distances to find suitable nesting sites and can lay multiple clutches per season.

Threats and Conservation

Spiny Softshell Turtles face severe threats in Ontario:

  • Habitat Loss: Riverbank alteration, dam construction, shoreline development
  • Nest Site Disturbance: Human recreation on nesting beaches
  • Water Quality: Pollution and sedimentation
  • Nest Predation: High rates of nest destruction by predators
  • Road Mortality: Deaths when females cross roads to nest
  • Small Population Size: Limited genetic diversity

Conservation efforts include habitat protection, nest site monitoring and protection, public education, and population studies. Several organizations are working to protect remaining populations and their critical habitats.

Legal Protection

The Spiny Softshell Turtle is listed as Endangered under both the federal Species at Risk Act (SARA) and Ontario's Endangered Species Act (ESA). This provides comprehensive protection, making it illegal to kill, harm, harass, capture, or possess this species.

Critical habitat protection is required under SARA, and recovery strategies must identify and protect essential habitat features. The species is also protected under Ontario's Fish and Wildlife Conservation Act, with additional regulations to prevent disturbance of nesting sites.
