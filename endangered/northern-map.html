Northern Map Turtle

Graptemys geographica

Quick Facts

  • Conservation Status: Special Concern (federally and provincially)
  • Size: 12-27 cm carapace length (females larger)
  • Weight: 0.5-2.5 kg
  • Lifespan: Up to 30+ years in the wild
  • Distinctive Features: Map-like pattern on shell, yellow markings on head and limbs

Description

The Northern Map Turtle gets its name from the intricate, map-like pattern of light lines on its olive to brown carapace (upper shell). These lines resemble contour lines on a topographic map. The plastron (bottom shell) is cream to yellow with dark lines along the scute seams.

They have a distinctive head pattern with yellow or greenish stripes behind the eyes. Females are significantly larger than males, with broader heads adapted for crushing their preferred prey. The carapace has a low keel (ridge) down the center with serrations along the back edge.

Habitat and Range

Northern Map Turtles inhabit large lakes, rivers, and reservoirs with clean water, abundant basking sites, and soft bottoms. They prefer areas with moderate currents and are rarely found in small ponds or stagnant waters. In Ontario, they are found primarily in the Great Lakes region and major river systems.

These turtles are excellent swimmers and spend most of their time in water. They require rocky or woody debris for basking and sandy or gravelly areas for nesting. Northern Map Turtles hibernate in mud or under debris at the bottom of water bodies during winter.

Related habitats: Lake Ontario, Lake Erie, St. Lawrence River, Rideau Waterway

Diet and Behavior

Northern Map Turtles are primarily carnivorous, with females specializing in mollusks (snails and clams) that they crush with their broad jaws and powerful bite. Males and juveniles eat more insects, crayfish, and other small aquatic invertebrates. They may also consume some aquatic vegetation and carrion.

They are strong baskers and can often be seen sunning on rocks, logs, or other debris, often in groups. Breeding occurs in spring, and females nest in late spring to early summer on sandy beaches or banks. Females may travel significant distances to find suitable nesting sites and typically lay one clutch per season.

Threats and Conservation

Northern Map Turtles face several threats in Ontario:

  • Habitat Degradation: Water pollution and shoreline development
  • Nest Site Disturbance: Human recreation on nesting beaches
  • Water Quality: Sensitivity to pollution and zebra mussel impacts on food sources
  • Road Mortality: Deaths when females cross roads to nest
  • Boat Strikes: Injuries from recreational boating
  • Nest Predation: High rates of nest destruction by predators

Conservation efforts include habitat protection, water quality improvement, nest site monitoring and protection, and public education. Their sensitivity to environmental changes makes them important indicators of aquatic ecosystem health.

Legal Protection

The Northern Map Turtle is listed as a species of Special Concern under both the federal Species at Risk Act (SARA) and Ontario's Endangered Species Act (ESA). This means that while not yet endangered or threatened, it may become so if protective measures are not taken.

It is illegal to hunt, trap, or possess Northern Map Turtles in Ontario without a specific permit. The species is also protected under Ontario's Fish and Wildlife Conservation Act. Management plans are being developed to address conservation needs.
