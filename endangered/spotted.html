Spotted Turtle

Clemmys guttata

Quick Facts

  • Conservation Status: Endangered (federally and provincially)
  • Size: 9-12 cm carapace length
  • Weight: 100-200 g
  • Lifespan: Up to 50+ years in the wild
  • Distinctive Features: Black shell and skin with bright yellow spots

Description

The Spotted Turtle is one of Ontario's smallest and most distinctive turtle species. It has a smooth, black carapace (upper shell) with numerous bright yellow spots that vary in number and pattern among individuals. The plastron (bottom shell) is yellow and black, and the head, neck, and limbs are black with yellow spots.

As they age, the yellow spots may fade or become less distinct. Males have brown eyes and a concave plastron, while females have orange eyes and a flat plastron. Their small size and attractive appearance make them vulnerable to illegal collection for the pet trade.

Habitat and Range

Spotted Turtles inhabit shallow, clean, slow-moving waters in wetlands, marshes, bogs, fens, and small streams. They prefer areas with soft bottoms, abundant aquatic vegetation, and plenty of basking sites. In Ontario, they are found in scattered populations primarily in the southern part of the province.

These turtles require a mosaic of wetland habitats and adjacent upland areas for nesting and seasonal movements. They are particularly sensitive to water quality and habitat disturbance. Spotted Turtles hibernate communally in wetland sediments or muskrat lodges during winter.

Related habitats: Lake Erie coastal wetlands, Sandbanks Provincial Park region

Diet and Behavior

Spotted Turtles are omnivorous, feeding on a variety of small aquatic invertebrates, insects, crustaceans, worms, aquatic vegetation, and algae. They forage primarily in shallow water, often walking along the bottom rather than swimming.

They are most active during spring and early summer, often becoming less active during hot summer months (aestivation). Breeding occurs in water during spring, and females nest in late spring to early summer in sandy or gravelly areas with good sun exposure. Spotted Turtles have small home ranges and show strong site fidelity.

Threats and Conservation

Spotted Turtles face critical threats in Ontario:

  • Habitat Loss: Wetland drainage and degradation
  • Habitat Fragmentation: Isolation of remaining populations
  • Illegal Collection: High demand in pet trade due to attractive appearance
  • Road Mortality: Deaths when moving between habitats
  • Nest Predation: High rates of nest destruction
  • Water Quality: Sensitivity to pollution and habitat alteration

Conservation efforts include wetland protection and restoration, population monitoring, public education, and enforcement against illegal collection. Several organizations are working to protect remaining populations and their habitats.

Legal Protection

The Spotted Turtle is listed as Endangered under both the federal Species at Risk Act (SARA) and Ontario's Endangered Species Act (ESA). This provides comprehensive protection, making it illegal to kill, harm, harass, capture, or possess this species.

Critical habitat protection is required under SARA, and recovery strategies must be developed and implemented. The species is also protected under Ontario's Fish and Wildlife Conservation Act, with additional regulations against collection and trade.
