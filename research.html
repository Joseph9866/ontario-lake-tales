Turtle Research

Advancing Turtle Conservation Through Science

Scientific research forms the foundation of effective turtle conservation in Ontario. Researchers across the province are working to understand turtle ecology, population dynamics, and the impacts of human activities on these ancient reptiles.

Featured Research Projects

Movement Ecology Studies

Using radio telemetry and GPS tracking to understand turtle movement patterns, home ranges, and migration routes across different landscapes.

  • Home range analysis
  • Road crossing behavior
  • Seasonal migration patterns

Nest Success Monitoring

Long-term studies tracking nest survival rates and factors affecting hatchling success in different habitat types.

  • Predation rates analysis
  • Temperature effects on development
  • Nest site selection studies

Population Genetics

Genetic studies to understand population connectivity, diversity, and the impacts of habitat fragmentation on turtle populations.

  • Genetic diversity assessment
  • Population connectivity
  • Inbreeding depression risks

Key Research Findings

Research shows that even low levels of road mortality (2-3% of adults annually) can lead to population declines in turtle species due to their slow reproductive rates. Studies have identified road mortality hotspots and demonstrated the effectiveness of ecopassages in reducing turtle deaths by up to 90%.

Temperature-dependent sex determination makes turtles vulnerable to climate change. Research indicates that warming temperatures could lead to skewed sex ratios, with potential impacts on population viability. Studies are monitoring nest temperatures and developing shading strategies to mitigate these effects.

Studies reveal that nest predation rates often exceed 90% in unprotected nests. Research has identified key predator species and demonstrated that nest protection programs can increase hatchling success to over 80%, significantly boosting population recruitment.

Research demonstrates that turtles require interconnected wetland complexes to complete their life cycles. Studies using movement tracking have shown that some species travel several kilometers between seasonal habitats, highlighting the importance of maintaining landscape connectivity.

Recent Publications

Population Viability Analysis of Blanding's Turtle in Fragmented Landscapes

Journal of Wildlife Management, 2023

Study demonstrating the importance of adult survival for population persistence and the effectiveness of road mitigation measures in maintaining viable populations.

Climate Change Impacts on Temperature-Dependent Sex Determination in Ontario Turtles

Canadian Journal of Zoology, 2023

Research on temperature-dependent sex determination and projected impacts of climate warming on Ontario turtle populations, with mitigation strategies.

Long-term Effectiveness of Road Ecopassages for Turtle Conservation

Conservation Biology, 2022

10-year study showing 85-95% reduction in road mortality following installation of turtle tunnels and fencing, with recommendations for optimal placement.
